When people talk about supplier quality, many still think first about incoming defects, rejection, claims, and 8D reports.  
Those are important, but if the work starts only after the material arrives, the problem has already happened.

From my experience on the manufacturing side, **supplier quality creates the most value before and during new product introduction, not after mass production problems have already appeared.**

## Why incoming inspection should not be the main focus

Incoming inspection is a defense line, but it is not where quality is truly built.

If a new project has not clarified the following points early enough:

- who defines the critical dimensions and special characteristics
- whether tooling and fixtures include effective mistake-proofing
- whether process capability can actually support the drawing requirements
- whether inspection criteria are clear enough to be executed consistently
- whether the supplier truly understands the major failure modes

then IQC can only intercept defects later. It cannot fundamentally reduce them.

That is why I see mature supplier quality management as something different from “blocking defects after they occur.”  
It is about **putting the right levers in place before the problem enters mass production.**

## The most important levers during NPI

### 1. Clarify the critical quality requirements early

Many supplier issues do not come from unwillingness. They come from inconsistent understanding at the beginning.

Typical problems include:

- no clear prioritization of critical drawing requirements
- no unified criteria for appearance, assembly, or function
- sample approval requirements mixed together with mass-production requirements
- customer-specific expectations not fully passed down

At the NPI stage, quality should not wait for samples to appear and then start pointing out issues.  
It should **align the quality language early.**

### 2. PFMEA must be used as a real prevention tool

In many companies, PFMEA becomes a document. In practice, it should be a working tool.

What matters most to me is whether it truly answers three questions:

- What is most likely to go wrong in this process?
- What will the customer experience if it fails?
- Can the current controls really detect it early enough?

If PFMEA remains only a scoring exercise, without returning to tooling, parameters, inspection methods, and process controls, the same problems will usually come back again.

### 3. Tooling and fixtures need early evaluation

Many batch problems look like operator mistakes, but the root cause is often in the tooling and fixture design.

If positioning is unstable, clamping is inconsistent, or the operation depends too much on individual experience, the process will remain unstable no matter how much pressure is put on execution.

That is why, during NPI, I pay close attention to:

- whether the fixture includes mistake-proofing
- whether the inspection fixture is stable enough
- whether the operation can be standardized
- whether the testing method balances efficiency and accuracy

In many cases, quality improvement does not mean adding another inspection step.  
It means making the process itself less likely to fail.

### 4. Sample approval must be linked to mass-production conditions

A good sample does not automatically mean a good production process.

Many projects perform well during sample submission but become unstable in volume production because:

- the sample condition is different from the real production condition
- the sample was selected, not produced consistently
- the process window was never validated
- the production inspection plan was not prepared in advance

For that reason, I prefer to treat sample approval as a verification of mass-production feasibility, not merely a step to pass customer confirmation.

## What quality should really do at this stage

I do not think quality should act only as an inspector during NPI.  
It should also act as:

- a risk identifier
- a process driver
- a standards clarifier
- an improvement coordinator

If quality only reacts after an issue appears, organizes meetings, assigns responsibility, and writes reports, its value stays limited.  
The more valuable work is to identify risks early and align suppliers, engineering, production, and inspection around the same execution rhythm.

## Closing thought

If I had to compress this note into one sentence, it would be this:

> The real goal of supplier quality management is not to catch incoming defects, but to absorb as many future mass-production problems as possible during new product introduction.

This is also the direction I want to keep writing about here:  
not generic statements about how important quality is, but practical levers, judgments, and lessons that actually matter on the manufacturing floor.
