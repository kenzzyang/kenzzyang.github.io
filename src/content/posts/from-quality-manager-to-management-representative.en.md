When people read a resume, they often see titles such as “Quality Manager,” “Management Representative,” “Assistant General Manager,” or “Executive Vice President” as separate positions.

But for me, these roles form one consistent path:

> I started from quality engineering, then gradually expanded into system building, shop-floor improvement, cross-functional coordination, and finally a broader understanding of what execution really means in manufacturing.

This article is not meant to simply restate my resume.  
It is meant to explain something more important:

**I have never seen quality as inspection alone, and I have never seen systems as paperwork alone. My work has always been about making quality requirements actually run on the shop floor and stay there.**

## My career path has been an expansion, not a series of disconnected moves

If I look back chronologically, my career can be understood in five main stages.

### Stage 1: Building the fundamentals at Defond Group

At **Dongguan Defond Group (1997.11–2012.6)**, I worked across roles such as **Senior Quality Engineer, Quality Manager, and Sales Engineer**.

The value of this period was not only in the titles themselves. It was that I learned the real basic operating system of quality work:

- quality standards and quality control plans
- customer complaints and supplier quality issues
- new-product trial follow-up
- quality communication and corrective-response handling
- day-to-day department coordination and team guidance
- translating customer requirements into internal execution

This was also the period when I built a practical foundation in:

- SPC
- QC seven tools
- 8D
- FMEA
- GR&R
- ISO9000 / ISO13485 / TS16949
- IPC-610-D and related standards

If I was later able to drive systems, processes, and cross-functional coordination, it was because this period taught me something essential:

**quality is not just about judging defects. It is about connecting standards, communication, verification, and improvement into one loop.**

### Stage 2: Moving from quality into broader operational coordination

At **Zhejiang Jiaben Electric Appliance Co., Ltd. (2012.6–2014.11)**, I worked as **Assistant General Manager**, while also taking part in quality management and serving as a **management representative**.

This was a major turning point for me. I was no longer looking at problems only from inside the quality department. I was increasingly looking at the company as an operating system:

- Were departments working in rhythm?
- Could abnormal issues be coordinated quickly?
- Could customer complaints, audits, processes, and equipment improvement be advanced together?
- Were documents and drawings actually usable in daily execution?

Some of the work I led or pushed in this period included:

- leading customer complaints and quality-system audits
- driving departmental performance and coordination
- improving production automation
- presiding over internal audits and management review
- improving process documents and abnormal-issue handling
- improving drawing and document sharing so departments could access current versions quickly

This period made one thing much clearer to me:

**if the quality team can only identify problems but cannot drive engineering, production, warehousing, and management to improve together, the same issues will return again and again.**

### Stage 3: Taking on broader site-execution responsibility

At **Zhejiang Changlong Electric Appliance Co., Ltd. (2014.11–2015.3)**, I served as **Executive Vice President**.

Although this period was not the longest, it gave me a more direct understanding of many issues that sit one level above “quality” in a narrow sense:

- how targets are set
- how manpower is allocated
- how processes become standardized
- how meetings and company activities are organized
- how site management is coordinated
- how efficiency and safety are improved together

Some representative actions from this stage included:

- driving technical departments to improve work instructions and drawings
- improving warehouse layout and material feeding arrangements
- introducing line-based operation and IE methods in production
- improving fixtures and automatic / semi-automatic equipment
- clarifying departmental responsibilities to reduce blame-shifting
- training and introducing new colleagues to help build a core team

This stage reinforced another important lesson:

**many problems that appear to be “quality issues” are actually process issues, responsibility issues, information issues, and execution issues.**

### Stage 4: Connecting systems, improvement, and execution at Snowpeak

At **Shaoxing Snowpeak Airgun Factory (2015.9–2020.9)**, I served as **Quality Control Manager, Assistant to the General Manager, and Management Representative**.

If the earlier stages expanded my scope, this was the stage where many of those capabilities were truly connected.

My responsibilities covered a relatively complete chain:

- daily quality-department operation
- quality-system revision and procedure improvement
- driving all departments to execute against system requirements
- supervising departmental targets and performance
- coordinating cross-functional issues
- establishing special improvement topics
- driving solutions to difficult production issues
- improving processes to raise quality and reduce cost

Representative outcomes from this period included:

- improving the company’s operating-system documents and passing **ISO9001 TUV** certification
- promoting digital reporting through quality monthly reports and management review
- improving supplier quality and FAI sample-evaluation requirements
- using semi-automatic dedicated tooling to improve barrel-test efficiency and accuracy
- promoting the use of mistake-proofing, safety tolerance, and DFMEA concepts through quality cases
- improving barrel-thread processes to reduce cost, improve precision, and develop new suppliers

This period made one belief much stronger for me:

**quality systems, quality tools, and shop-floor improvement only become valuable when they are connected.**

If the system remains only on paper, it becomes a burden.  
If improvement has no standards or data behind it, it becomes experience-driven and unstable.  
If there is no coordination mechanism, cross-functional work cannot last.

### Stage 5: Moving quality further upstream into suppliers and NPI

At **JTS (Hangzhou) Technology Co., Ltd. (2020.9–2024.6)**, I worked as **Quality Manager**, with a stronger focus on **supplier quality, new-product development control, customer confirmation, and feedback improvement**.

This stage shifted my attention even more clearly upstream.

My responsibilities included:

- supplier new-product development and sample follow-up
- driving process quality control during production preparation
- coordinating customer sample approval and feedback improvement
- arranging shipment inspection and report review for batch products
- improving outgoing inspection and PQR standardization
- following up technical and quality feedback from sales and after-sales
- continuously driving supplier quality improvement

This period deepened one key insight:

**many mass-production problems are actually NPI problems that were never fully addressed early enough.**

That is why I increasingly emphasize APQP, PFMEA, inspection planning, sample logic, and consistency between sample conditions and production conditions.

### Stage 6: Bringing quality further forward in a more complete manufacturing chain

At **Forcome (Zhejiang) Co., Ltd. (2024.6–present)**, I am serving as **Senior Quality Manager / QE Senior Manager**, working within a fuller manufacturing chain for vehicle-related products.

An important part of my work now includes:

- quality participation in new-product development
- approval of parts, process samples, and finished-product samples
- quality control plans, PFMEA, and APQP support
- tooling and testing-equipment evaluation
- inspection-plan development
- supplier quality improvement
- customer complaint response and improvement follow-up

What I care about most is not waiting for problems to appear and then reacting.  
It is exposing hidden risks as early as possible during trial build and introduction—for example by identifying tooling risks early, promoting mistake-proofing, and summarizing trial issues before they become batch problems.

## What these stages finally built in me

If I compress this path into a few stable capability areas, I would describe them like this.

### 1. I understand not only quality judgment, but how systems and processes actually land

I have worked as a quality engineer, quality manager, management representative, and coordination role near the operating center of the business.  
That means I see quality systems not as audit material, but as tools for stable execution.

### 2. I do not only identify problems; I drive cross-functional closure

Most quality issues cannot be solved by the quality department alone.  
Real improvement usually requires suppliers, engineering, production, warehousing, and customer requirements to be aligned together.

### 3. I look beyond inspection and focus on tooling, process, and prevention

I put strong emphasis on moving problems upstream.  
Instead of relying only on more inspection, I focus on:

- whether tooling is mistake-proofed
- whether the process is stable
- whether the risk is identified early
- whether sample conditions are truly linked to production conditions

### 4. I connect quality work with efficiency, cost, safety, and execution

Quality does not stand alone.  
It becomes truly effective only when it is connected with efficiency, cost, safety, process discipline, and organizational execution.

## How I now understand the role of management representative

Many people see the role of **management representative** as something document-heavy, audit-heavy, or system-heavy.

But in my own career, I have come to believe that the value of this role is not really in the word “representative.”  
Its value is in the word **connection**.

It connects:

- system requirements with shop-floor execution
- quality goals with departmental action
- management review with daily improvement
- cross-functional communication with accountability

In other words, **if a management representative cannot translate system requirements into shop-floor action, the role becomes hollow. If that translation is done well, the role becomes highly valuable.**

## Closing thought

Looking back, my own conclusion is becoming clearer:

> I am not a single-type quality professional. I have moved through quality engineering, quality management, system building, shop-floor improvement, and operational coordination, gradually forming a way of working that emphasizes execution, coordination, and upstream prevention.

That is also the direction I want to keep building on this website:  
not only listing job titles, but slowly organizing the methods, judgments, and practical lessons behind them.
